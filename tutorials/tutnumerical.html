Hexadecimal Number

Octal and hexadecimal numbers have a considerable advantage over our decimal numbers in the world of bits, and is that their bases (8 and 16) are perfect multiples of 2 (23 and 24, respectively), which allows us to make easier conversions from these bases to binary than from decimal numbers (whose base is 2x5).

For example, suppose that we want to translate the following binary sequence to numbers of other bases: 110011111010010100

In order to translate it to decimal we would need to conduct a mathematical operation similar to the one we have used previously to convert from hexadecimal, which would give us the decimal number 212628.

Binary Number

Octal and hexadecimal numbers have a considerable advantage over our decimal numbers in the world of bits, and that their bases (8 and 16) are perfect multiples of 2 (23 and 24, respectively), which allows us to make easier conversions from these bases to binary than from decimal numbers (whose base is 2x5).

For example, suppose that we want to translate the following binary sequence to numbers of other bases: 110011111010010100

In order to translate it to decimal we would need to conduct a mathematical operation similar to the one we have used previously to convert from hexadecimal, which would give us the decimal number 212628.

Nevertheless to pass this sequence to octal it will only take us some seconds and even the less skilled in mathematics can do just b seeing 8 is 23, we will separate the binary value in groups of 3 numbers: 110 011 111 010 010 100

giving the number 637224 as result. This same process can be inversely performed to pass from octal to binary.
